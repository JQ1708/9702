1. Physical Quantities

What is a physical quantity?

Speed and velocity are examples of physical quantities; both can be measured.

  • ‚úÖ All physical quantities consist of a numerical magnitude and a unit.
  • In physics, every letter of the alphabet (and most Greek) is used to represent these.
  • To represent a physical quantity, it must contain both a numerical value and the unit.

Example: The letter v can represent:

  • Velocity (Unit: m s-1)
  • Volume (Unit: m3)
  • Voltage (Unit: V)

The units provide the context!

Question: Estimate the energy required for an adult man to walk up a flight of stairs.

Recall the equation for gain in gravitational potential energy:

ΔEp = m g Δh

Here, g is approximately 10 N kg-1.

  • An adult person mass m ≈ 70 kg
  • A flight of stairs height h ≈ 3 m
Diagram: Man on Stairs

The energy required is approximately:

ΔEp = 70 √ó 10 √ó 3 = 2100 J

Tip: Mark schemes are generous with estimate ranges.

Homogeneity of Physical Equations

We use base units to check if an equation is physically correct. Units on Left = Units on Right.

üìù Worked Example: Homogeneity

Question: The speed of sound v is given by v = √γ pρ. Show that γ has no units.

Speed v = m s-1

Pressure p = FA = N m-2 = (kg m s-2) m-2 = kg m-1 s-2

Density ρ = mV = kg m-3

Let units of γ be G.

m s-1 = √G √ó kg m-1 s-2kg m-3

Simplify fraction: kgkg cancels. m-1m-3 = m2.

m s-1 = √G m2 s-2 = √G √ó m s-1

Therefore, √G = 1, so G = 1 (No units).

3. Scalars & Vectors

Scalars

Quantities that have MAGNITUDE but NO DIRECTION.

Examples: Mass, Distance, Speed, Time, Energy, Volume, Density, Pressure, Electric Charge, Temperature.

Vectors

Quantities that have BOTH MAGNITUDE AND DIRECTION.

Examples: Weight, Displacement, Velocity, Acceleration, Force, Momentum.

Distance vs Displacement

Distance (Path) vs Displacement (Straight Line)
  • Distance: Total length of path taken. (Scalar)
  • Displacement: Straight line length and direction from start to finish. (Vector)

Combining Vectors

Two methods: Triangle Method and Parallelogram Method.

Triangle Method (Head-to-Tail)

  1. Link vectors Head-to-Tail.
  2. Resultant connects tail of first to head of second.

Equilibrium

If forces are in equilibrium, they form a CLOSED VECTOR TRIANGLE.

Resolving Vectors

A single vector F can be split into vertical and horizontal components.

x = F cos θ Fy = F sin θ

Precision vs Accuracy

Calculating Uncertainty

  • Reading (e.g. Voltmeter): ± half the smallest division.
  • Measurement (e.g. Ruler): ± at least 1 smallest division (due to two ends).
  • Repeated Data: ± 12(Range).
  • Digital Reading: ± last significant digit.

Example: Mean 12.50 cm, Range 0.10 cm.
Result: 12.50 ± 0.05 cm.

Combining Uncertainties

Adding / Subtracting

ADD the ABSOLUTE uncertainties.

If y = a - b

Uncertainty Δy = Δa + Δb

Multiplying / Dividing

ADD the PERCENTAGE uncertainties.

If v = dt

%Δv = %Δd + %Δt

Powers

MULTIPLY % uncertainty by power.

If V = r3

%ΔV = 3 √ó %Δr
